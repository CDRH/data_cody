      <div class="note">Buffalo Bill's Wild West</div>



      <span class="dateline">Brooklyn<a href="../doc/n01" class="internal_link">1</a>
         <br/>

May 9th<a href="../doc/n02" class="internal_link">2</a>
      </span>

      <span class="salute">My Dear <span class="persName">George</span>
      </span>


      <p>Have you sent the Bond back to Mr Clark<a href="../doc/n03" class="internal_link">3</a> 
         <a rel="tooltip" class="sic" title="it's​ ">its</a>
    ​ necessary he has it soon as possible. Say that contract with Wallenburg<a href="../doc/n04" class="internal_link">4</a>— does it state when Wallenburg must pay for land? May be we can get him on that clause— Let me know what Sisters<a href="../doc/n05" class="internal_link">5</a> first payment on her 160 is &amp; I will send the money to you</p>

      <p>Business is great with me.</p>

      <p>When did you get water in town do you think you can keep it coming?</p>


      <span class="closer">
         <span class="salute">Your friend</span>  


         <br/>W F Cody</span>

   </div>





   <div class="note">Note 1: Buffalo Bill's Wild West performed in Brooklyn, New York, May 5-10, 1902.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 2: The year is not inscribed by Cody but is 1902.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 3: "Mr Clark" may be E. H. Clark, financial manager for Phoebe Hearst, from whom Beck had secured a loan for Shoshone Irrigation Company.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 4: "Wallenburg" is likely Gustav Wallenburg, a Swedish immigrant from Chicago, who worked as a land agent in the Big Horn Basin; in 1907 Wallenburg filed but lost a lawsuit against Shoshone Irrigation Company.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 5: "Sisters" is a reference to the payment owed by Helen Cody Wetmore (1850-1911), William F. Cody's sister.</div>
