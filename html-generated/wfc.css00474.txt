      <span class="salute">My Dear <span class="persName">George</span>—</span>

      <p>I have not heard from you since wire from Chicago. Please send me a wire per Pony Express<a href="../doc/n03" class="internal_link">3</a> to Red Lodge<a href="../doc/n04" class="internal_link">4</a>—so I may know if water is in Ditch. George— <span class="persName">Rumsey</span> said this was no longer a one man Co. If so you must write— Paxton &amp; Gallagher.<a href="../doc/n05" class="internal_link">5</a> Lininger &amp; Metcalf<a href="../doc/n06" class="internal_link">6</a> &amp; others that if they <a rel="tooltip" class="sic" title="do not​ ">donot</a>
    ​ not want to trust our <u>Co</u> for goods you will buy where they will trust Co— You should not ask me to guarantee everything personally as was done again this time

   <div class="note">Note 1: Cumberland, Maryland: Buffalo Bill's Wild West performed in Cumberland, Maryland, on April 27, 1896. </div>

   <div class="note">Note 2: The year is not inscribed by Cody but is 1896 as determined by the Routes List. The day's entry in the 1896 Route Diary: "Cumberland, Md. / Monday, April 27. It commenced to rain at 2 P. M. and kept it up until 8.30 P. M. Albert E. Scheible, business manager, closed here and left on the 8.30 train for New York City. J. Byrns, an Irish soldier, was taken sick with pneumonia and went to the hospital. Lot at Valley Spring and Fairview avenues, clay and bad to pick up on. Got out of Cumberland at 2 A. M. / Arena, 173 x 344. / Business in the afternoon big, in the evening fair."</div>

   <div class="note">Note 3: "Pony Express" is Cody's way of saying send a wire as quickly as possible.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 4: Red Lodge, Montana, lies 65 miles north of Cody and was the closest rail line to Cody, Wyoming.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 5: Paxton-Gallagher is a wholesale groceries business, founded in Omaha in 1879 by <span class="persName">William Paxton</span> and Benjamin Gallagher.</div>

   <div class="note">Note 6: Lininger &amp; Metcalf: George Washington Lininger (1834-1901) and Joseph M. Metcalf (1846-1905) were partners in the Lininger &amp; Metcalf Company, an Omaha wholesaler of carriages, buggies, and agricultural implements. </div>
